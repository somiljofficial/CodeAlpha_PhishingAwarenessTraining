# Phishing Awareness Training

## 1. What Is Phishing?
Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where an attacker impersonates
a trusted entity — a bank, coworker, delivery service, or IT department — to
trick a victim into revealing sensitive information (passwords, OTPs, card
details) or performing an action that benefits the attacker (clicking a link,
transferring money, installing malware).

## 2. Common Types of Phishing
- **Email phishing** – mass emails pretending to be from a trusted brand
- **Spear phishing** – targeted, personalized attacks on a specific person
- **Smishing** – phishing via SMS/text messages
- **Vishing** – phishing via phone calls
- **Business Email Compromise (BEC)** – impersonating an executive/coworker to request money or data

## 3. How to Recognize Phishing Emails
Look out for:
- **Urgency or fear language** — "Your account will be suspended in 1 hour"
- **Mismatched sender domains** — e.g. `paypa1.com` instead of `paypal.com`
- **Generic greetings** — "Dear Customer" instead of your name
- **Requests to bypass normal process** — "Don't tell your manager, just send the codes"
- **Unexpected attachments or links**
- **Slightly altered URLs** — extra characters, subdomains, or number substitutions

## 4. How to Recognize Fake Websites
- Check the URL carefully before entering any credentials
- Look for HTTPS, but note: HTTPS alone does NOT guarantee a site is legitimate
- Be suspicious of pop-ups asking for login details
- Verify the domain matches the real company's official domain exactly

## 5. Social Engineering Tactics Used by Attackers
Attackers rely on a few psychological levers:
- **Authority** – pretending to be a boss, bank, or government official
- **Urgency** – creating a false deadline to prevent careful thinking
- **Scarcity** – "Only 1 spot left" or "Limited time offer"
- **Familiarity/Trust** – impersonating a known coworker or friend

## 6. Best Practices to Avoid Phishing
1. Verify suspicious requests through a **separate, trusted channel** (call the bank using the number on your card, not the number in the email)
2. **Hover over links** before clicking to check the real destination
3. Never enter credentials via a link from an email — go to the site directly
4. **Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** wherever possible
5. **Report** suspicious emails to IT/security instead of just deleting them
6. Keep software and browsers updated

## 7. Real-World Style Examples (for illustration)
- A text claiming "Your package couldn't be delivered, click here to reschedule" with a suspicious short link
- An email claiming to be from HR asking you to "verify your payroll details" via an external form
- A call from someone claiming to be from your bank's fraud department, asking you to read out an OTP
